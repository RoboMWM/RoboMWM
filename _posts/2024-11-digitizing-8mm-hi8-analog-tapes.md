The audience.

I've seen a lot of bad advice, especially on YouTube, on digitizing old camcorder analog tapes! At least, if your intention is to archive these memories with some degree of quality. It's likely that if you've kept old camcorder tapes around, you likely have other hardware that can easily improve the quality.

This info is compiled from hours of forum posts. When I started putting this together I did not source the posts I've used - nor do I consider myself any form of an expert given the testing and experience of those who have done this for years. I also am using hardware that I already have available - my only purchase was the USB capture device, as the existing Dell Movie Studio I have has some weird interlacing issue (which there is a term for, but I have forgotten) at the top of its frame.

My setup is not going to get you the _best_ quality, but it will provide acceptable quality that will provide a better degree of quality than most of the YouTube tutorials out there, and without spending hundreds of dollars. I'm also assuming NTSC as I'm an American and will be catering to other fellow Americans.

I expect that you've arrived here likely looking for a way to digitize some old ANALOG camcorder tapes. These instructions are only intended for digitizing old analog camcorder tapes, such as 8mm and Hi8 tapes. VHS-C is also analog, but I haven't digitized these myself yet.

If you have MiniDV tapes, do NOT use this guide! Mini DV tapes contain DIGITAL data and use a separate method.

The hardware setup.

First, I use the original camcorder. You will need a 3.5mm to RCA breakout cable. If your camcorder has an S-VIDEO out, get an S-VIDEO cable as well - please use S-VIDEO instead of the yellow composite cable! (I've seen too many youtube tutorials completely ignore this port!) My Sharp VL-E720U unfortunately doesn't have an S-VIDEO port, so I have to use the yellow composite cable.

Next, we will connect the camcorder to a DVR. No, we aren't recording on the DVR, and no, just not any DVR. If you happen to have a Panasonic DMR-ES10, ES15, or EH55 lying around like I do, you will plug the camcorder into its input. I just use input 1 on the back. The DVR will serve as a TBC-ish, meaning that it will correct _some_ timing related issues with analog tapes. If you do not have a Panasonic DMR-ES10, ES15, or EH55 lying around, then these can be had from eBay, with the ES10 being the best of these three. We will go over configuring this DVR later after we have everything connected up.

Lastly, we need a capture card of some sort to connect the S-VIDEO cable and audio RCA cables to your computer. There are many options out here, but whatever you do, don't cheap out on a no-name chinese brand, nor the Elgato. Reasonably British has a video reviewing some of these options, with those two being the worst. I actually have a Dell Movie Studio Plus card, but it seems there's next to zero talk about this card online, and I ended up not using it because it had some weird stuff going along the top of its capture. Not sure if this affected the S-VIDEO input either, but I decided to just buy the I-O Data GV-USB2 so I could use my newer Windows 10 PC to capture the footage.
